    Day 11 [Tue. July 15]
    ---------------------

    This may be one small step for you guys, but it's a big step for me. I finally got all my levelling done with the 3/4" modified crushed stone base. At least its as as good as I'm going to get (see picture 1). Maybe now I can start getting to the fun stuff.

    I ordered 1.5 tons of sand that will be delivered in the morning (thanks for the push Adam). To get ready for this, we put the second course of wall block up. To prep for the adhesive, my son had the chore of brushing off the powdery dust that some of the Coventry wall block has and using the compressor to shoot air at it (picture 2). Thanks to Allied for the tip. We then laid the individual blocks and got the whole 20' face to fit from column to column. When this was done, we just picked each one up, applied the adhesive, and put it back in place.

    Tomorrow's plan is to start screeding the sand, laying a couple of pavers and cutting a few. I'll probably go slow (nothing new there) as I try to get a feel for all of this.

    Thanks,

    Jim K.

    Jim, I think it's great you are taking on such a project! We worked with Devonstone a few times this year and last. A couple tips I want to add. The last picture you show really has to wide of a gap to fill properly with polysand. A perfect angle will make the nice radius you spent so much time on seem choppy. To achieve a nicer curve measure from the corner towards the patio towards each corner and at a 45 degree from the same corner. Get 3 points and rough draw a line between the 3 points. Then cut using a cut saw or grinder. EP recommends using propak instead of bedding sand, but we use concrete sand with no issues. Another main point I'd suggest... use the gatorsand over polysand. Gatorsand is a meant for larger joints. We find ourselves going back to fix the polysand with Devonstone when we used regular polysand (meant for closer joints)

    Jim,

    I think you have thought that one out pretty well. The only concern I would have is how the cuts will match up with one another when placed in succession. Having two cut stones match with each other is very important. What about this for an idea - use your method, except add a half inch above your line and instead of running your cuts to the corners, run them to a half inch above the corners. This will allow you to get your stone in against the wall. From there you can use your center pin to mark your radius. Just take the radius of the wall and subtract your joint and mark the stone that way. Then use a 4 1/2" angle grinder to cut out the curve. You can mark each stone with the same radius measurement. That way you have a smooth consistent inner joint.

    Also, I would recommend using Alliance XP for the joints. It's rated to 1 1/2" and not as gritty as their Gator Dust. I used it today with flagstone and it worked like a charm. Here's a link http://alliancedp.com/

    So, I'm cutting the very first paver to go in, and my son comes out and says "Dad, there's a storm warning and the radar shows a big red spot heading directly at us". I was torn between thinking "I'm not stopping now. Its sunny out and I want to start laying pavers!", and "I'm not going to have a storm ruin all this sand".

    As you can see in the picture, the fear of the storm won out. We wound up adding a lot more stones on the tarp than in the picture because they were predicting high winds. They also mentioned hail which I figured no tarp was going to help with. The big red spot on the weather radar just disappeared as soon as it reached my area. We did get a little rain after some of the winds, so it was worth the effort. Plus, now that we've gone through this drill, we know how get the tarp set up pretty quick. However, I hope I did this right, I didn't have much time to think it through. I don't know what to expect when I unwrap it tomorrow...

    The last picture is the one paver I was able to cut. This was as far as I got. I was just about to lay it on the sand and check the fit against the wall when I got the warning. I found myself trying to rush because I really wanted to get to that milestone. Than I stopped and remembered what I usually tell my kids "the faster you go, the longer it takes". Usually because you wind up doing it over a couple of times.

    So, what do the professionals do to change the weather?

    Jim,

    If you want to use a plate tamper, you could. Just get two or three sheets of plywood (1/2") and put that down on top of the devonstone. Just have your son shift around one while you tamp on the others. The plywood will spread out the impact of the plate tamper evenly on all the stone so you get an equal distribution of force. We use this method with Travertine and it works fine.....and I know travertine will break a lot easier than devonstone. I've also used this method with Blue Mountain Stone, which is essentially the same as devonstone. Just a thought.
