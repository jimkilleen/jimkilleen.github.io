    Originally Posted by JimK
    This is as far as I got today. One of the things I need to do is recalculate how many inches of stone face..... so I can avoid cutting. starting in the center was correct (if for some reason you do get out of level it won't be as noticable) I am not sure what block your using but generally you use the largest block for the bottom(buried) then you can start adding in the other sizes.

    ... Will I be able to run the compactor near the block, or do I now risk moving them out of position? I plan to add crushed stone on both sides of the block. Can I run the compactor over both the crushed stone and the wall block, or can this cause damage?...
    yes you can use a compactor, this is what you are supposed to do with AllenBlock wall. just use common sense, pour your crusher in the back and front before compacting and you don't need to compact on the block itself since it is not a hollow core unit.

    Originally Posted by JimK
    ....after compacting the base, there's still going to be imperfections in the height and how level it is. ... is it okay to thow some loose stones underneath, hit with a mallet to get it right and move on to the next one? ....
    I screed out stonedust to help level my walls, and get yourself a deadblow rubber mallet. its filled with little ball bearings that slam the mallet home without any bounce back. makes short work of leveling.

    Originally Posted by JimK
    I had planned to cover any work in progress overnight once I start working with the sand (holding off yet on that delivery). Are you supposed to do that for the crushed stone as well? Is the concern that it weakens the base?.
    you don't need to cover the crusher base stone, just remember you don't tamp it if its puddling, wait till it dries out some. Now the bedding sand is a different story, that must be dry and kept that way till you've finished the job.

    Originally Posted by JimK
    ... in a previous response you also mentioned having 3 10' ers and a couple 5' ers as well. What is the significance of that combination? In other words how do you lay them out? Also, I probably can't screed the whole thing in a day, so how do you handle doing a couple 10' sections, laying the pavers, and then screeding from where you left off the next day and keeping the levels even? I haven't gotten this far yet, but with the large area I have to cover, I'm wondering how to do this..
    I think what I said was get three poles and have the third cut into half. This is what I consider to be the minimum. I have a lot more than that but that combination will get you by.

    I screed with my landscape rake so I put my poles 3' apart. take your 4' level and check from pole to pole, if you need to adjust use roofing shingles(I bought an open bag at HD for $5) cut into 1 1/2" by 3" strips. if you need to use more than 2 shingles to make level that area needs more base stone. if your slightly high sometimes you can pound your pole into the base an 1/8". now that your level from pole to pole (the length of the pole will be running down your slope so that won't be level) shovel sand directly over the top of the poles one in the middle and one near the top and bottom. That step just keeps them from moving on you. then shovel or carefully dump sand inbetween but not too much higher than the poles (just don't dump a full wheelburrow in one spot). Also don't pull the sand past the end of the poles. Then pick up the outside pole an move it over so you can screed sand on the otherside of the middle pole. once this is done fill in your pole trenches with more sand and scrape level with the rest of the bed.

    Since your using a circle kit (I think) start with your circle so you end up with a 10' screeded square. get a piece of plywood to walk over your sand so you can start laying the circle kit, when you've gotten to the edges now you can lay the poles down and screed more sand in workable sections.

    it only took me 45 minutes to lay my circle kit patio with edges, it really is the easiest and quickest part.

    I would run the compactor a few times in a checkerboard fashion. Overlap a little each pass. Go back and forth one way, then alternate the other direction. Going in circles the whole time may cause you to have some waves in your base. Also, you may want to use your poles as you come up to final level. I lay mine out and check for the high and low spots using my screed poles. I also place two poles adjacently, 6 or 7 feet apart and then span them with my screed board and throw a level on top of that to check my level. Also use a level directly on the poles to double check your pitch every direction.

    You can screed your top layer of base if you want forego the tweaking, but I was saying to use the poles as more of an indicator. You can use the poles to look for low spots (anywhere the poles aren't touching) and add a little here and there. You also use the poles (or the board) to increase the size of your 4 foot level to a 10 foot level by placing the level on top. Use this method to check level over a larger area. Use two poles and a level bridged in between to check for pitch.

    Just little tricks to get to get around not having a laser level. Strings set up in quadrants will be the most accurate.

    Jim

    I wouldn't use method 1. If you try and mark out the radius of the circle first, you are likely to be off when you lay the circle. You can lay your circle first if you want, or lay your pattern and leave room for your circle. If you lay your pattern all the way around your circle, then you can use a plank and span the space where the circle is going so you can start in the middle without walking on the sand. As far as cutting in by the wall....this is what separates the men from the boys in our field. This is where the craftsman end of hardscaping comes in. A few tips would be to not worry so much about tracing the wall onto the stone. You want to measure the distance between the where the paver will start and where it will end at the wall. Measure both ends of the stone and mark them. From there you can mark a curve between those points. Over exagerate your curve a little, then after you cut it, you can fine tune it with an angle grinder. If you really want to go nuts, you could always make a template of the curve. You can use a thick gauge wire, or what I've done in the past is to take a piece of edging and piece of string, and loop a string through a spike hole for the edging on each end, and tighten the string to pull the edging into a curve. Adjust it to match the curve of the wall. I just eye my cuts and mark them that way, but that takes time to get good at, so the template may be the way to go.

    Jim,

    PL landscape block adhesive is fine for a free standing wall, for all the courses and the cap. It's might not be the best adhesive available, but it is more than sufficient for your application.

    Definitely glue all courses.

    You should get 10-15 linear ft. out of a 10oz tube, 25-30 linear ft. from a 29oz tube.

    Use a thin bead of glue in a zig-zag pattern, keep it away from the face of the block so the excess doesnt ooze out when you put the block together. Set the block in place, wiggle it around alittle, lift it off a few inches to let airate the adhesive then set it back in place.

    Also, you're using coventry wall, it can be pretty dusty/powdery. Use a stiff brush and a blower or air compressor to clean the surface off before gluing.
